What's that symbol?

Logical operation

Last Updated: 1 January 2023

A logical operation transforms individual propositions into compound propositions. Most logical operations are analogues to natural English expressions such as "not," "and," "or," and "if."

Here's the order in which logical operations should be evaluated:

  1. Parentheses
  2. Negation (\(\lnot\))
  3. Conjunction (\(\land\))
  4. Disjunction (\(\lor\))
  5. Any other logical operation (Parentheses should be used to give order)
Contents

Negation

Negation is a logical operation that acts on only one proposition and reverses its truth value. The negation of a proposition \(p\) is denoted \(\lnot p\) and read as "not \(p\)".

\(p\) \(\lnot p\)

Conjunction

Conjunction is a logical operation that acts on two propositions and results in a compound proposition that is true if and only if both of the starting propositions were true. The conjunction of propositions \(p\) and \(q\) is the proposition \(p \land q\) and is read "\(p\) and \(q\)". It is true only if both \(p\) and \(q\) are true, and false if \(p\) is false, \(q\) is false, or both \(p\) and \(q\) are false.

\(p\) \(q\) \(p \land q\)

"p and q," "p but q," "despite p, q," and "although p, q" are all conjunctions of p and q.

Disjunction

Disjunction is a logical operation that acts on two propositions and results in a compound proposition that is false if and only if both of the starting propositions were false. The disjunction of propositions \(p\) and \(q\) is the proposition \(p \lor q\) and is read "\(p\) or \(q\)". It is false only if both \(p\) and \(q\) are false, and true if \(p\) is true, \(q\) is true, or both \(p\) and \(q\) are true.

\(p\) \(q\) \(p \lor q\)

Exclusive disjunction

Exclusive disjunction is a logical operation that acts on two propositions and results in a compound proposition that is true if and only if exactly one of the starting propositions is true. The exclusive disjunction of propositions \(p\) and \(q\) is the proposition \(p \oplus q\) and is read "\(p\) or \(q\)". It is true only if just \(p\) is true or just \(q\) is true, and false if both \(p\) and \(q\) are true, or both \(p\) and \(q\) are false.

\(p\) \(q\) \(p \oplus q\)

Implication

The conditional operation, implication, is a logical operation that acts on two propositions and results in a compound proposition (a conditional proposition) that is false if and only if the first proposition, the hypothesis, is true, while the second proposition, the conclusion, is false. The implication of propositions \(p\) and \(q\), where \(p\) is the hypothesis and \(q\) is the conclusion is the proposition \(p \rightarrow q\) and is read "if \(p\) then \(q\)". The proposition \(p \rightarrow q\) is false if and only if \(p\) is true and \(q\) is false, otherwise, it's true.

\(p\) \(q\) \(p \rightarrow q\)

Biconditional

The biconditional operation is a logical operation that acts on two propositions and results in a compound proposition that is true if and only if the starting propositions are logically equivalent. The biconditional operation applied to propositions \(p\) and \(q\) results in the proposition \(p \leftrightarrow q\) which is read "\(p\) if and only if \(q\)". The proposition \(p \leftrightarrow q\) is true if and only if \(p \equiv q\).

\(p\) \(q\) \(p \leftrightarrow q\)
